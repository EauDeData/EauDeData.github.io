Abstract

Decentralization as an opportunity, not a tax

Privacy constraints keep archives, governments, and small businesses from pooling document data. Federated learning is usually treated as a necessary evil — a price paid for privacy. This paper argues the opposite: train models separately on each silo, average their weights, and the merged model often generalizes better — especially out-of-distribution and in low-resource regimes.

Centralized vs distributed — drag the slider

Pool everything Keep apart, merge

This is the first study to evaluate distributed learning in Document Analysis along three axes at once: the tasks (word spotting, handwriting/OCR, table recognition), the architectures (RNNs, transformers, graph networks), and the fine-tuning strategies (distillation, layer-wise, end-to-end).

Chapter 1 · Introduction

Why the data can't move

Modern training assumes you can pile all the data in one place. For documents — sensitive administrative records, cultural-heritage collections meant to stay near their origin, confidential SME paperwork — that assumption simply fails. Distributed learning trains where the data lives and shares only the resulting weights.

The threefold hypothesis
i · BETTER TEACHERS
Merged features are more general, so they distill better into small student models.
ii · MULTI-SCRIPT
General enough to absorb entirely new alphabets, even far from the training distribution.
iii · BETTER INIT
A superior starting point for subsequent in-distribution fine-tuning.

"Decentralization is not merely a constraint, but a valuable opportunity to improve model robustness and adaptability."

Chapter 2 · Framing

One principle, many names

Model soups, task arithmetic, model-agnostic meta-learning, FedAvg — they look different but share one move: train models on different units, aggregate them afterwards without sharing data (Eq. 3). This work treats them as one family and asks where it pays off in Document Analysis.

Chapter 3.1 · Methodology

Model merging, in weight space

A model is just a vector of weights θ ∈ ℝm. Train it on the whole dataset and you get the centralized θD. Train one model per silo and average them — that's the distributed θ̄D. The data never leaves its silo; only the weight vectors are shared.

θ̄D  =  (1/n) · Σi=1…n θdi     (Eq. 3 — the move behind FedAvg, task arithmetic & model soups)

Drag the silo optima — watch the merged model form

Each silo trains independently from the pretrained init θ⁰ to its own optimum θdi (dashed paths). The distributed model is literally their average (orange ★). Toggle to see what centralized does instead: pool the raw data into one pile and train a single model to the combined-loss basin.

Same compute, same data seen

Decentralizing is not more expensive

A common objection: surely n separate trainings cost more? No. Centralized sees N points for T epochs = N·T steps. Splitting N = n₁+n₂+… into silos gives n₁·T + n₂·T + … = (n₁+n₂+…)·T = N·T. Product is distributive — the budgets are identical. Drag to re-split the data.

Chapter 6 · When it wins

A decision map, not a prescription

The paper's title is a targeted call. Distributed learning is not a universal winner — it dominates in specific regimes. Describe your situation and see which side the evidence favors.

Chapter 6 · Conclusions

A targeted call

DATA-CENTRIC
Best in low-resource regimes

Largest gains on the smallest data (M96, 50 pages) and on independently-learned new alphabets — the gap narrows once data is abundant.

ARCHITECTURE-CENTRIC
Best when few params train

Strongest zero-shot and with light heads (GRU/LSTM); degrades under full end-to-end fine-tuning of heavy models.

DOMAINS
Heritage & SMEs

Scarce annotations, distribution shift, and privacy/access constraints align precisely with where distributed learning helps.

PRIVACY FOR FREE
No data leaves the silo

The same compute, the same data seen — only weights are shared, and often the merged model is better.

Decentralization in Document Analysis is not a universal prescription, but a targeted call — to practitioners under low-resource, low-adaptability, or deployment-constrained conditions, for whom distributed learning is competitive and often superior.
