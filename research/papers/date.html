The reframing

Three ways to ask "when?"

A classifier forces every photo into a decade bin and treats a one-year miss the same as a fifty-year miss. A regressor predicts a single number and is brittle far from its training mass. This paper takes a third route: train the network to rank a gallery by date-closeness to a query, so a wrong-but-near answer is still a good answer.

classification · regression · retrieval — hover the paradigms
Retrieval returns an ordering: even a near miss lands close, giving the archivist a usable "older / newer" answer.

"In the worst case, when the exact date is not estimated, the user can obtain a relative ordering — one photograph is older than another — which is useful in archival annotation."

Why a new metric · Section 3

mAP can't tell a near miss from a far one

Years are ordinal. mAP only knows "relevant / not relevant", so to it a photo one year off and one twenty years off are identical — both just "wrong". nDCG instead uses a graded relevance r(n) and a logarithmic discount, rewarding rankings that put the closest dates on top. Swap two near years and mAP often doesn't budge — it already counted both as "relevant". nDCG moves, because it cares how the good answers are ordered and how the bad ones are spread through the list. That sensitivity to graded, ordinal ground truth is the paper's whole motivation.

Making it differentiable · Eq. 6–8

A sigmoid stands in for the step

Rank is a sum of hard comparisons 𝟙{sᵢ − sⱼ < 0} — counting how many items beat i. That indicator has no gradient. The trick (Qin et al.): replace it with a sigmoid G(x; τ) = 1 / (1 + e^(−x/τ)). Small τ hugs the true step (accurate but a near-flat gradient); large τ is smooth and trainable but blurrier. Drag τ to feel the trade-off (this is Fig. 1).

Designing relevance · Eq. 15–17

What counts as "close enough"?

nDCG needs a relevance score for every gallery item. The choice shapes the embedding: a hard clip says "anything within a decade is equally good"; an inverse or exponential law keeps pressing for the exact year. Pick a relevance function and see its profile against the year gap |y_q − y_n|.

Training loop · Algorithm 1

Embed → rank all-vs-all → score → back-propagate

No labels-as-targets here. A batch goes through a ResNet-101 into embeddings; an all-versus-all cosine matrix gives every image its ranking against the others; the relevance matrix (from the years) says what the ranking should be; smooth-nDCG measures the gap and the gradient flows back into the CNN. Conclusions

Ranking beyond categories

ORDINAL GROUND TRUTH
Graded, not binary

Unlike smooth-AP, smooth-nDCG scores the whole sorted list — how good the good ones are, and how the bad ones are spread.

CONTEXTUAL ANSWER
A ranking, not a point

Even a missed year yields an "older/newer" ordering against the collection — what archivists actually need.

SWAPPABLE PARTS
Any domain, any distance

Change the relevance matrix and the same loss transfers to other ordered or tree-structured retrieval tasks.

CAVEAT
Category bias

Unbalanced classes (trains, vehicles) can cluster by object rather than date — the embedding inherits dataset skew.

Brown et al. smoothed the path to average precision; smooth-nDCG extends it to problems where different labels should not be punished equally — ranking images by a continuous, ordered criterion rather than a categorical one.
