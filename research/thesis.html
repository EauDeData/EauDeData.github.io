Chapter 1

The Aristotelian AI Problem

On the three-way tension between discourse, truth, and measure — and why historical documents fundamentally resist large-scale pretraining.

LLMs privilege what sounds persuasive over what is true. Retrieval systems offer evidence without articulating relationships. Evidence itself has been devalued in favour of discourse.
— Introduction, p. 2
The Oldest Quarrel — Plato, Protagoras, c. 380 BC

Persuasion vs. the short, answerable question

The tension this thesis confronts is twenty-four centuries old. In Plato's dialogue, the sophist Protagoras answers in makrologia — long, beautiful speeches that persuade without ever being pinned down. Socrates keeps interrupting to demand brachylogia: short answers, one question at a time, each one checkable. Toggle the two modes and watch what happens to persuasion versus knowledge actually established.

PERSUASION—
KNOWLEDGE ESTABLISHED—

Rhetoric maximises persuasion while establishing almost nothing; dialectic trades eloquence for things you can actually check. The modern chatbot is Protagoras with a server farm.

How We Got to the Triangle — a lineage of the three forces

From the agora to the search bar

Each era leaned on a different corner of the same triangle. Click along the timeline: the sophists sold discourse; dialectic and, later, the scientific record chased truth; the index, the archive, and the search engine industrialised measure; the LLM looped back to discourse at planetary scale. The thesis asks for all three at once.

The Triadic Tension — since Socrates & Protagoras

Discourse, Truth, Measure

Two and a half millennia after Socrates asked the sophist Protagoras for brachylogia — short, specific answers instead of persuasive rhetoric — we have not escaped the same three-way pull. Drag the marker: every information technology sits somewhere inside this triangle. The chatbot leans into discourse; the classic search engine offers measure without relation; the thesis asks whether a system can hold all three at once.

A Vessel of Information — Fig. 1.3, p. 8

The anatomy of a document

A document is a record of human activity fixed on a durable medium. The Manual of Archival Science separates it into three layers: the material support that gives it physical body, the record that captures information onto that support, and the information itself — the shapes, words, and people confined within. Hover each layer, then explode the stack to see them apart.

Layer 01 · Material support
The substrate
Papyrus, magnetic film, a sheet of copper — the raw physical body, prior to any acquisition.
Layer 02 · Record
The means of capture
The ink on paper, the light fixed in a photograph, the hammer-strike imprinting a pattern.
Layer 03 · Information
The witness
The signature, the people in the frame, the inscription — the testimony of human activity itself.

In this thesis, both photographs and textual documents count as documents — as long as the context of application is archival.

Retrieval is the task of identifying relevant information in a corpus, conditioned on a signal. Before the contributions, the shared vocabulary: queries, ranking, and the long tail that breaks conversational shortcuts.

Conditioning a Corpus — Ch. 2, p. 17

A signal pulls relevance out of a collection

Every retrieval system maps a query-signal onto a ranking of documents. Type a query, or pick one, and watch the corpus reorganise: relevant items rise, the threshold τ draws a line, and the long tail of almost-relevant material trails off below it — the very tail a chatbot quietly hides.

Bars above τ are retrieved. The shaded tail below is what a comprehensive system must still acknowledge as possibly relevant.
Precision & Recall — the eternal trade-off

The two ways to be wrong

Move the threshold above and the two classic measures pull against each other. A loose threshold retrieves everything — high recall, low precision. A strict one is pure but blind to the tail. The thesis argues a historical engine must, above all, acknowledge the absence of false negatives — knowing what it failed to find.

The PHOC Fingerprint — Pyramidal Histogram of Characters

How a word becomes a position-aware fingerprint

Word spotting compares words by a descriptor, not by their literal characters. The PHOC records which letters occur and, recursively, where — the whole word, then halves, then thirds. Two OCR variants of the same word land close together in this space. Type a word and watch its pyramid build.

try: justicia · sanidad · colera
Level 1 sees the whole word; deeper levels split it, so two words that share letters but not order are pulled apart.

The Swapping Trick — Figs. 5.3–5.4, p. 118

Separate what from when — then transplant time

A single embedding tangles an object's category with its era. The training pipeline pulls them apart: a proxy loss builds year-agnostic category clusters, subtracting the category mean µᶜ centres every cluster into one shared temporal subspace, and the leftover residual is the era itself. The swapping trick then translates that residual onto another object — letting you ask "this jacket, as it would look in 1950," a query no label could phrase. Step through the decomposition.

Chapter 7

Conclusions

Four research questions, four answers. A technical framework for Aristotelian AI in historical document retrieval.

RQ1 → ANSWERED
Grounded & Measurable
Model-checking with visual routines provides traceable, verifiable retrieval. Each result is anchored to logical predicates — not opaque similarity scores. The system can explicitly state why an image was rejected.
RQ2 → ANSWERED
A Distributed Foundation
Task Arithmetic / FedAvg consistently outperforms centralised pretraining in low-resource and out-of-distribution regimes. The Borg cipher goes from 0% to 50.5% accuracy. Data never leaves its archive.
RQ3 → ANSWERED
Date as Archival Proxy
TDIR enables date-sensitive composed queries that mirror archivist reasoning. An 8× improvement over CLIP in date precision confirms that temporal structure must be explicitly learned, not hoped for.
RQ4 → ANSWERED
Temporally Coherent Embeddings
The swapping trick enforces orthogonal category/year subspaces as an emergent property of joint optimisation — formally proven via mutual information. Cars from 1940 and 1990 stay semantically linked.

The ideal search engine for historical archives is not a text generator. It is a measurable, relational, comprehensive system — one that counts relationships, constrains literals, acknowledges the absence of false negatives, and preserves the doubt that makes historical inquiry honest.
